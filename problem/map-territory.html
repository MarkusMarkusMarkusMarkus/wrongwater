The map is not the territory.

A (mental) "map" is a mental representation of reality: the structured set of concepts, distinctions, expectations, and inferred relations we use to navigate the world with. It is what allows us to keep track of things even when they are not directly present to perception.

Importantly, the map is not the territory—our internal model of what is is different from what is the case: we can be mistaken, confused, deceived…

The "territory" stands for reality: the world as it is, independent of our representations of it. The map is a compressed, selective, simplified reconstruction of that same world. It leaves things out, highlights some features instead of others, and organizes experience into usable patterns.

This is not a flaw but a necessity. A map that included everything would no longer be a map at all — it would be as unwieldy as reality itself.

This compression, however, what makes maps both useful and vulnerable. Useful because we could not think, speak, or act without it. Vulnerable because any map can misrepresent the territory: carving reality at the wrong joints, importing false distinctions, or inheriting distortions from the language it is built out of.


social-reality
The power of social reality is that it determines what is treated as real.

Consider a bank run. What is a bank run? It's what happens when enough people stop treating a bank as safe and start acting on that changed belief. And then, suddenly, the bank is in trouble. A shift in shared maps with immediate material consequences.

Shared maps help determine not just which banks remain liquid but which companies are viable, which institutions retain legitimacy, who counts as a ruler, who the president is, who commands the army, and who gets obeyed. Social reality is not separate from power. It helps organize authority, compliance, and coordination.

It is not an accident that revolutionaries so often seize television stations, radio towers, and now social media channels. Power depends not only on force, but on controlling the shared maps through which force, legitimacy, and authority are recognized.

That is why struggles over public language, symbols, and shared belief are never merely symbolic. They are struggles over the map — and therefore over the social world organized through it.
