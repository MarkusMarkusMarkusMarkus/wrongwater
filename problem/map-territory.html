The map is not the territory.

A (mental) "map" is a mental representation of reality: the structured set of concepts, distinctions, expectations, and inferred relations we use to navigate the world with. It is what allows us to keep track of things even when they are not directly present to perception.

Importantly, the map is not the territory—our internal model of what is is different from what is the case: we can be mistaken, confused, deceived…

The "territory" is reality: the world as it is, independent of our representations of it. Our "map" is a compressed, selective, simplified reconstruction of that same world. It leaves things out, highlights some features instead of others, and organizes experience into usable patterns.

This is not a flaw but a necessity: a map that included everything would no longer be a map at all — it would be as unwieldy as reality itself.

This compression, however, makes maps both useful and vulnerable. Useful because we could not think, speak, or act without it. Vulnerable because any map can misrepresent the territory: carving reality at the wrong joints, importing false distinctions, or inheriting distortions from the language it is built out of.
