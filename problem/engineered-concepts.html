Problem Solution

Engineered concepts do not just label reality. They reorganize it in advance.

"Politically correct." "Microaggression." "Toxic masculinity."

These aren't just recent words. They are recent concepts. And concepts are a different kind of tool — they don't just frame an existing thing, they carve up a domain and determine what you're even looking at.

Engineered concepts are deliberately introduced or spread in order to shape how a domain of reality is perceived, organized, and judged.

One common mechanism: creating a compound term that imports the associations, legitimacy, or emotional force of one concept into another.

  • "Politically correct" suggests not just political acceptability, but correctness.
  • "Microaggression" extends the force of aggression to smaller, subtler acts.
  • "Toxic masculinity" binds a negative evaluative term to a broader social category.
  • "Vegan beef" borrows the associations of beef while referring to something that is not beef.
  • "Social justice" imports the moral force of justice into a more specific ideological package.

The trick is not exactly that these terms are false — concepts are neither true nor false. It's that the concept is built so that part of the conclusion is smuggled into the term itself. Instead of neutrally describing a phenomenon, the concept pre-structures how it will be interpreted.

Compare to engineered names (relabeling) and engineered words (recoloring). Concepts redraw the map entirely.
