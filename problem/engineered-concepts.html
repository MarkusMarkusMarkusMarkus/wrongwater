Engineered concepts do not just label reality. They reorganize it in advance.

"Politically correct." "Job creators." "Collateral damage."

These aren't just recent terms. They are recent concepts. And concepts don't just frame an existing thing: they carve up a domain and shape what you even think you're looking at.

Engineered concepts are concepts that are deliberately introduced and spread in order to shape how a domain of reality is perceived, organized, and judged.

One common mechanism for their creation is to create a compound term that imports the associations, legitimacy, or emotional force of one concept into another.

  • "Politically correct" suggests not just political acceptability, but correctness.
  • "Job creators" extends the moral weight of creation and productivity to a specific economic class.
  • "Collateral damage" imports the clinical neutrality of engineering into what would otherwise be called civilian deaths.
  • "Microaggression" extends the force of aggression to smaller, subtler acts.
  • "Social justice" imports the moral force of justice into a more specific ideological package.

The trick is not exactly that these terms are false — concepts are neither true nor false. It's, rather, that the concept is built so that part of the desired conclusion is smuggled into the term itself. Instead of neutrally describing a phenomenon, the concept pre-structures how it will be interpreted.
