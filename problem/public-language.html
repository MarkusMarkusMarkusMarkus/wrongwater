Most of the language you use, you did not invent.

"Public language" is the language that is publicly available: language acquired from outside ourselves. That is most of the language we use — how many of the words you've said today did you personally coin?

And it makes sense that you didn't: if each person had to invent their own words, we'd collapse into a Tower of Babel scenario. Publicly available language allows shared thought across many minds enabling communication and coordination.

However, because we do not think in wholly self-invented language, but instead in language inherited from, and shared with, many others, the medium we think with can be strategically shaped before it reaches us. And, in fact, the larger the number of people whose thinking depends on a particular shared linguistic medium, the stronger the incentive to do so.

This matters because shaping shared language helps shape shared maps, and shared maps help allocate status, legitimacy, coordination, and power. Language is therefore also a strategic terrain.

Public language, reappropriated for private thinking, is therefore not just a neutral medium of thought. It is also a contested coordination medium — which is why it gets engineered.
