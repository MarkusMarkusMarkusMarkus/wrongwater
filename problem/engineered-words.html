You do not always need to change what people are looking at.

"He is firm." "He is obstinate." "He is pig-headed."

These three sentences do not point to three different traits. They point to the same trait, but with different (and increasingly negative) evaluative coloring.

Words point, but they also frame. They carry emotional valence, implied judgments, social cues. Two words can refer to the same thing while pulling very different feelings — and therefore very different inferences — from us.

Which is why engineered words are powerful. You don't even need to change what people are looking at to change how they react to it. Sometimes it's enough to change the word through which they're looking.

Engineered words often work not by changing denotation, but connotation. When that works: two distinct terms can share the one and same referent while having very different emotional and evaluative fields around them, leading people to think and act differently about the same thing.
