You do not always need to change what people are looking at.

"He is firm." "He is obstinate." "He is pig-headed."

These three sentences do not point to three different traits. They point to the same trait, but with different (and increasingly negative) evaluative coloring.

Words point, but they also frame. They carry emotional valence, implied judgments, social cues. Two words can refer to the same thing while pulling very different feelings — and therefore very different inferences — from us.

Which is why engineered words are powerful. You don't even need to change what people are looking at to change how they react to it. Sometimes it's enough to change the word through which they're looking.

Engineered words often work not by changing denotation, but connotation. When that works: two distinct terms can share the one and same referent while having very different emotional and evaluative fields around them, leading people to think and act differently about the same thing.

Recolouring the map

denotation-connotation
Words do two things at once: they point and they suggest.

A word's denotation is what it directly refers to. In the case of "tiger," its denotation is that large orange-and-black striped cat out there in the world.

But words also have connotations: the feelings, associations, judgments, and implied meanings that gather around them. In the case of "tiger," these might include danger, fear, wildness, predation — the fact that this is the thing that ate your cousin.

A word does not merely pick out a pattern. It also carries a halo of associations around that pattern. Over time, words tend to accumulate more and more of these associations. They may strictly refer to one thing, but they come to suggest much more than that thing alone.

This creates an opportunity for language engineering. One way to engineer language is to create a new word to refer to an old thing so as to avoid the connotation of the old word for it. The thing being referred to is the same but, with a new word, you can change the emotional and evaluative field around it. Like magic.
