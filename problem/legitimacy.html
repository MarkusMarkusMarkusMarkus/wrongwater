Problem Solution

Legitimacy is a social equilibrium, not a private opinion.

Authority is the socially recognized capacity to direct action. Legitimacy is a stable structure of interlocking expectations that such authority is rightful.

A ruler is not legitimate merely because some individuals privately approve of them. They are legitimate when enough people regard their authority as rightful, expect others to regard it as such, and act accordingly. These higher-order expectations help stabilize obedience, deference, and coordination.

Which is why legitimacy can persist even after private belief has begun to erode. People may stop personally believing in a ruler's rightfulness while continuing to comply, because they still expect others to comply. What holds the system together is often not conviction alone, but the expectation of others' conviction and behavior.

That is also why legitimacy can collapse so quickly. Once mutual expectations begin to shift, public behavior can change with startling speed.

interlocking-expectations
Social orders often decay slowly in belief but collapse quickly in public reality.

Social systems can shift very quickly because what holds them together is often not just private belief, but higher-order expectations. People do not merely act on what they personally think: they act on what they think others think, and on what they expect others will do.

A regime, norm, institution, or public consensus may therefore remain stable even after many people have stopped personally believing in it. First-order belief weakens quietly, but public behavior continues unchanged, because everyone still expects everyone else to go along.

This creates the possibility of long periods of apparent stability masking gradual erosion underneath. What looks solid may in fact be an equilibrium held together only by mutual expectation.

That is why social change often has tipping-point behavior. Once people begin to realize that others no longer believe either — or no longer intend to comply — the higher-order expectation structure can flip very quickly. And when that flips, behavior can change with startling speed.

This is one reason controlling public language matters so much. Public language does not merely express what people think. It helps shape what people think others think — and therefore the expectation structures that hold social reality together.
