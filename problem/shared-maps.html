Problem Solution (Upcoming) Map About

Social life depends on maps that overlap across many minds.

A map is a mental representation of reality. A map is shared when several people use the same concepts, words, symbols, and distinctions that that map is made of.

When that happens, when there is sufficient overlap between maps, communication, coordination, and social life become possible. We start inhabiting a shared world.

Social reality, thus, depends heavily on shared maps. And maps are transmitted through public language: words and symbols let concepts spread across minds; overlapping concepts let overlapping maps emerge.

This is why shaping public language can shape social reality. Whoever can shift the shared map — by introducing a new concept, relabeling an existing category, or changing what something feels like — can shift what counts as real, legitimate, or worth acting on.

social-reality
Social reality has power because it determines what people treat as real.

Consider a bank run. What is a bank run? It is what happens when enough people stop treating a bank as safely liquid and start acting on that change in belief. And then, suddenly, the bank is in trouble. A shift in shared maps has immediate material consequences.

Shared maps help determine which companies remain viable, which institutions retain legitimacy, who counts as a ruler, who the president is, who commands the army, and who gets obeyed. Social reality is not separate from power. It helps organize authority, compliance, and coordination.

It is not an accident that revolutionaries so often seize television stations, radio towers, and now social media channels. Power depends not only on force, but on controlling the shared maps through which force, legitimacy, and authority are recognized.

That is why struggles over public language, symbols, and shared belief are never merely symbolic. They are struggles over the map — and therefore over the social world organized through it.
