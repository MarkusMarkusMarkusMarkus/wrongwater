In social reality, shared maps make the territory.

There is an old adage: the map is not the territory. It is a useful corrective against confusing what we think is the case with what actually is the case. It applies well to material reality, which is indifferent to our thoughts about it.

But social reality works differently. Money, laws, offices, borders, reputations, and institutions are real — but they are real only because people collectively treat them as real. The shared map is not a representation of the territory. It is the territory.

That is why struggles over language, concepts, and public meaning are never merely symbolic. In domains where shared maps constitute reality, maps do not describe the world — they are how the world gets made.
