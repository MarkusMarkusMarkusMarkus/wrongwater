Problem Solution

In social reality, shared maps constitute the territory.


The idea that "the map is not the territory" applies especially well to material reality — which is indifferent to our thoughts about it. 

But social reality works differently. Social reality depends on shared maps: on sufficiently aligned beliefs, concepts, expectations, symbols. Money, laws, offices, borders, reputations, and institutions are real, but they are real partly because people collectively treat them as real and expect others to do so as well.

In material reality: the map is not the territory. In social reality: shared maps constitute the territory.

That is why struggles over language, concepts, and public meaning matter so much: in domains where shared maps help build reality, maps do not merely describe the world — they are how the world gets made.

Most of the language you use, you did not invent.

"Public language" is the language that is publicly available: language acquired from outside ourselves. That is most of the language we use — how many of the words you've said today did you personally coin?

And it makes sense that you didn't: if each person had to invent their own words, we'd collapse into a Tower of Babel scenario. Publicly available language allows communication, coordination, and shared thought across many minds.

But that means it is not optimal for reasoning.

Because we do not think in wholly self-invented language, but instead in language inherited from, and shared with, many others, the medium we think with can be strategically shaped before it reaches us. And the larger the number of people whose thinking depends on a particular shared linguistic medium, the stronger the incentive to do so.
