Political struggle can be — and is — carried out through language.

Political struggle is conflict over the terms of collective life: who holds office, how we allocate resources and status, who has legitimacy, and what are the rules under which we live together.

Such struggle can be carried out through force or formal institutions. But it can also extend into conflict over the shared maps through which social reality is organized.

When that happens, public language becomes the arena of that struggle — it supplies the labels, concepts, and frames through which people perceive and coordinate.

Importantly, this struggle is not symmetrical. Some actors can broadcast labels and frames at scale while most others primarily receive them and exchange them amongst themselves. A single broadcaster reaches millions of people. Millions of people each reach only a few.

This asymmetry matters because whoever shapes the public signal disproportionately shapes the shared maps — and therefore shapes social reality itself.
