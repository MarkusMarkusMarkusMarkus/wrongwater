Material reality is that which, when you stop believing in it, does not go away.

Philip K. Dick defined reality as "that which, when you stop believing in it, doesn't go away." Mountains, water, air, sunlight — neither of these care about what you think about them.

Social reality isn't like that. It is made from collectively recognized facts — money, laws, the Emperor's power, borders, marriage, job titles, corporations, calendars. Social reality exists only insofar as we believe that it does.

There are Iron Age tribes living in the Amazon who don't believe in banks and, therefore, don't have banks. You could airlift a bank and drop it in the middle of their village but you wouldn't really expect that, should you come back in a few years, the tribespeople would all be dressed as bank tellers. The belief is missing. Material reality has been replicated. But social reality has not — because their beliefs are all the same as they were before.

Importantly, material reality survives universal withdrawal of belief while social reality does not.

This matters because it means social reality can be constructed — and therefore contested. Whoever shapes the shared maps shapes what counts as real, binding, and worth acting on. See also: the map is the territory.

map-is-territory
In social reality, shared maps constitute the territory.

There is an old adage that the map is not the territory. It is a useful corrective against a common mistake: confusing what we think is the case with what actually is the case.

It applies especially well to material reality — which is indifferent to our thoughts about it. Mountains do not disappear when we stop believing in them. Gravity does not weaken because we don't know about it.

But social reality is different. Social reality depends on shared maps: on sufficiently aligned beliefs, concepts, expectations, and symbols. Money, laws, offices, borders, reputations, and institutions are real, but they are real partly because people collectively treat them as real.

So in material reality, the map is not the territory. In social reality, shared maps constitute the territory.

That is why struggles over language, concepts, and public meaning matter so much: in domains where shared maps help build reality, maps do not merely describe the world — they are how the world gets made.
