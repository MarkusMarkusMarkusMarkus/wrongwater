Social reality shifts first slowly, then all at once.

Social reality can shift very quickly because what holds it together is not just private belief but higher-order expectations. People do not merely act on what they personally think: they also act on what they think others think, and on what they expect others will do.

A regime, norm, institution, or public consensus may therefore remain stable even after many people have stopped personally believing in it: first-order belief weakens quietly, but public behavior continues unchanged, because everyone still expects everyone else to go along.

This creates the possibility of long periods of apparent stability masking gradual erosion underneath. What looks solid may in fact be an equilibrium held together only by mistaken mutual expectation.

Because of this social change often has tipping-point behavior. Once people begin to realize that others too have stopped believing in the previous order — or no longer intend to comply with it — the higher-order expectation structure can flip very quickly. And when it flips, behavior can change with startling speed.

That is one reason controlling public language matters so much. Public language does not merely express what people think. It helps shape what people think others think — and therefore the expectation structures that hold social reality together.
