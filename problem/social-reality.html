The power of social reality is that it determines what gets treated as being real.

Consider a bank run. What is a bank run? It's what happens when enough people stop thinking of a bank as safe and start acting on that changed belief. And then, suddenly, the bank is in trouble. A shift in shared maps has immediate material consequences.

Shared maps help determine not just which banks remain liquid but which companies are viable, which institutions retain legitimacy, who counts as a ruler, who the president is, who commands the army, and who gets obeyed. Social reality is intrinsically connected to power: it helps organize authority, compliance, and coordination.

It is not an accident that revolutionaries so often seize television stations, radio towers, and now social media channels. Power depends not only on force, but on controlling the shared maps through which force, legitimacy, and authority are recognized.

That is why struggles over public language, symbols, and shared belief are never merely symbolic. They are struggles over the map — and therefore over the social world organized through it.
